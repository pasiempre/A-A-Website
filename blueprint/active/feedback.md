# Part 1: Persona Deep-Dive

Before designing pages, you need to understand who's landing on them, what triggered their visit, and what will make them convert. This section gives you the foundation the page architecture is built on.

---

## Persona A: General Contractors

### Who They Are
- **Title:** Project Manager, Superintendent, or Owner/Operator at a GC firm
- **Company size range:** 5-person local GC up to regional firms running 10+ concurrent projects
- **Age/demo:** Typically 30-55, male-dominated field, action-oriented communicators
- **Day-to-day:** Managing multiple trades on a construction timeline, dealing with punch lists, coordinating inspections, fielding calls from owners and architects

### When They Search for a Cleaning Sub
**Trigger moments:**
1. They have a project nearing closeout and their last cleaning sub flaked, delivered inconsistently, or is unavailable
2. They're bidding a new project and need to line up a reliable cleaning sub for the scope
3. They just got a punch-list callback specifically about cleanliness and need it resolved fast
4. They're scaling their operation and need a go-to cleaning partner rather than scrambling every time

**Search behavior:**
- Google: "post construction cleaning Austin", "final clean subcontractor Austin TX", "construction cleanup company near me"
- Referral: asked another GC, another sub, or their supplier rep
- Direct: saw the truck on a jobsite, got a card at a trade event

### What They Care About (Ranked)

| Priority | Concern | What It Really Means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Schedule reliability** | "Will they show up when they say they will? My CO date doesn't move." |
| 2 | **Quality on first pass** | "I don't want to re-clean or get punch-listed on cleanliness. One pass, done." |
| 3 | **Insurance & licensing** | "If they break something or someone gets hurt on my site, am I exposed?" |
| 4 | **Scope understanding** | "Do they know what rough clean vs. final clean actually means? Or will I have to babysit?" |
| 5 | **Communication** | "Can I reach them? Will they text me back? Do they speak the language my crew speaks?" |
| 6 | **Price** | Important but NOT #1. GCs will pay more for reliability. They've been burned by cheap subs. |

### Their Objections
- "I already have a guy" → they need a reason to switch or add a backup
- "How do I know you'll show up?" → proof of schedule reliability
- "Can you handle my scale?" → multi-unit, multi-story, multi-trade coordination
- "What if the walkthrough fails?" → re-clean policy, zero-punch-list guarantee
- "Are you insured enough for my site?" → COI specifics, coverage limits

### Language They Use
- "Punch list", "CO" (certificate of occupancy), "closeout", "walkthrough", "rough clean", "final clean"
- "Trades", "subs", "super" (superintendent), "PM"
- "Handoff", "turnover", "TCO" (temporary certificate of occupancy)
- They talk in timelines and deadlines, not in feelings

### What Convinces Them
- **Another GC's endorsement** — nothing beats peer proof
- **Seeing your crew on an active jobsite** — they evaluate competence visually
- **Fast, professional response** to their initial inquiry (proves communication quality)
- **A COI ready to send** without being asked
- **Specific scope knowledge** — knowing the difference between Phase 1 and Phase 2 clean

## Persona B: Property Managers

### Who They Are
- **Title:** Property Manager, Regional Operations Manager, Leasing Manager, Asset Manager
- **Company type:** Multifamily management company, HOA management, or the property management arm of a real estate firm
- **Manages:** Apartment complexes (100-500+ units), mixed-use properties, senior living communities
- **Day-to-day:** Lease renewals, maintenance tickets, vendor coordination, inspections, resident complaints, budget management

### When They Search for a Cleaning Vendor
**Trigger moments:**
1. Turnover volume spikes (summer move-out season, lease cycle end) and their internal maintenance team can't keep up
2. Current vendor delivered inconsistent quality — units failing leasing inspections
3. New property acquisition — need to establish vendor relationships from scratch
4. Common areas (lobby, hallways, amenity spaces) look tired and they need a reset

**Search behavior:**
- Google: "apartment turnover cleaning Austin", "move out cleaning service commercial", "property management cleaning vendor Austin"
- Referral from other PMs at industry meetups (Austin Apartment Association, IREM chapters)
- Vendor directories or property management software integrations

### What They Care About (Ranked)

| Priority | Concern | What It Really Means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Turnaround speed** | "I have 3 days between move-out and move-in. Can you turn a unit in 24-48 hours?" |
| 2 | **Consistency across units** | "I need every unit to hit the same standard, every time, whether it's unit 101 or unit 412." |
| 3 | **Scheduling flexibility** | "Can you work around resident schedules, leasing tours, and maintenance?" |
| 4 | **Pricing predictability** | "Give me a per-unit rate I can budget against. No surprises." |
| 5 | **Inspection readiness** | "Will the unit pass a pre-move-in inspection the first time?" |
| 6 | **Communication & reporting** | "Can you tell me when it's done? Can I track status?" |

### Their Objections
- "We handle it in-house" → they need to see the math on outsourcing vs. internal labor cost
- "We need consistent pricing" → they want flat-rate or per-unit pricing, not hourly
- "Can you handle volume?" → seasonal spikes mean 20+ units in a week
- "What if the unit doesn't pass?" → re-clean guarantee
- "We need someone who won't disrupt residents" → scheduling sensitivity, noise, access protocols

### Language They Use
- "Turn", "turnover", "make-ready", "unit prep"
- "Lease-ready", "inspection-ready", "showing-ready"
- "Common areas", "amenity spaces", "model units"
- "Per-unit", "per-turn", "run rate"
- They think in unit counts and occupancy rates

### What Convinces Them
- **Consistent portfolio results** — "we clean X units per month across Y properties"
- **Speed metrics** — "average 24hr turnaround per unit"
- **Before/after of a specific unit type** (studio, 1BR, 2BR)
- **A reference from another PM** at a comparable property
- **Clear pricing structure** they can drop into their operating budget

## Persona C: Commercial Space Managers

### Who They Are
- **Title:** Facilities Manager, Office Manager, Operations Director, Building Manager
- **Company type:** Office buildings, retail centers, medical/dental offices, schools, churches, co-working spaces
- **Manages:** Active occupied spaces where cleaning happens around business operations
- **Day-to-day:** Vendor management, tenant relations, building maintenance, budget oversight, compliance

### When They Search for a Cleaning Vendor
**Trigger moments:**
1. Current cleaning company let quality slip — complaints from tenants or employees
2. New lease/new tenant moving in — need the space presentation-ready
3. Post-renovation or post-construction cleaning needed before re-occupancy
4. Seasonal deep clean requirement (end of year, pre-audit, health inspection)

**Search behavior:**
- Google: "commercial cleaning Austin", "office cleaning service Austin TX", "janitorial service commercial"
- Referral from building owner, tenant, or facility management peer
- RFP process (larger operations)

### What They Care About (Ranked)

| Priority | Concern | What It Really Means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Minimal disruption** | "We're open for business. Clean around us, not through us." |
| 2 | **Reliability & consistency** | "Show up every Tuesday and Thursday. Same standard every time." |
| 3 | **Professional presentation** | "My space represents my brand. It needs to look managed, not just cleaned." |
| 4 | **Flexible scheduling** | "Evenings, weekends, off-hours — whatever works around our operations." |
| 5 | **Health & safety compliance** | "Especially post-COVID. We need sanitization protocols, especially for medical/dental." |
| 6 | **Scalability** | "Can you add deep cleans quarterly? Can you handle our expansion to a second location?" |

### Their Objections
- "We already have a janitorial service" → they need a reason to switch (usually quality decline)
- "We need someone who understands our industry" → retail vs. medical vs. office have different needs
- "Can you work off-hours?" → 90% of commercial cleaning happens outside business hours
- "What's your cancellation policy?" → they want flexibility, not long-term lock-in
- "Do you do both regular and deep cleans?" → they want one vendor for both

### Language They Use
- "Janitorial", "facility maintenance", "deep clean", "day porter"
- "Tenant", "occupant", "common areas", "high-traffic"
- "Walk score" (how the space presents to visitors)
- "SLA" (service level agreement), "scope of work"
- They think in frequency (daily, weekly, monthly) and zones (lobby, restrooms, offices)

### What Convinces Them
- **Consistency proof** — "we've served X client weekly for Y years"
- **Off-hours capability** — "our crews work evenings and weekends"
- **Industry-specific experience** — showing you know the difference between cleaning an office and cleaning a medical suite
- **A walkthrough offer** — "let us assess your space and propose a scope"
- **Flexible contract terms** — month-to-month available, not just annual

# Part 2: Page Architecture

This is the recommended module structure for each industry page. The flow follows the same trust funnel logic as your homepage but compressed and persona-specific.

---

## Recommended Page Flow

```
1. Hero (persona-specific value prop + dual CTA)
2. Pain Acknowledgment (what's not working in their current situation)
3. How A&A Solves It (3-4 tailored solution points)
4. Recommended Services (linked cards with proof lines)
5. Social Proof Block (testimonial + stat bar)
6. Before/After (persona-relevant example)
7. How It Works (3-step simplified process)
8. FAQ Cluster (3-5 persona-specific questions)
9. Conversion Block (form or CTA)
10. Footer
```

Below is the detailed spec for each module.

## Module 1: Hero

**Purpose:** Immediately confirm the visitor is in the right place and communicate the primary value proposition.

### What "Standard" Looks Like
- Generic headline ("Cleaning for Property Managers")
- Stock photo background
- Single CTA

### What "Optimal" Looks Like
- Persona-specific value prop headline
- Relevant background image (real jobsite photo)
- Dual CTA (quote + call)
- 1-2 trust signals below CTAs

### What "High-End" Looks Like
- Dynamic headline that could reference their sub-industry or location
- Real project photography from A&A's portfolio
- Micro-stat in the hero ("200+ closeouts completed on schedule")
- Trust bar immediately below (licensed, insured, response time)

### Recommended Headlines by Persona

| Persona | Headline | Subtitle |
|---|---|---|
| **General Contractors** | **Closeout-Ready Cleaning. On Your Schedule.** | Post-construction rough and final cleans for Austin GCs who can't afford a callback. |
| **Property Managers** | **Turnover-Ready Units. Every Time.** | Fast, consistent move-in/move-out cleaning that keeps your leasing pipeline moving. |
| **Commercial Spaces** | **Clean Spaces That Run Like Your Business.** | Ongoing and deep-clean support for offices, retail, and facilities across Austin. |

### Spec
- Background: real A&A project photo relevant to persona (construction site for GC, clean apartment for PM, polished office for commercial)
- Overlay: dark gradient, 60-70% opacity (lighter than current — let the image contribute)
- Kicker above headline: "FOR GENERAL CONTRACTORS" / "FOR PROPERTY MANAGERS" / "FOR COMMERCIAL SPACES"
- Headline: serif or semi-bold display, 40-48px desktop
- Subtitle: 16-18px, max 2 lines
- CTAs: "Request a Quote" (primary) + "Call (512) 555-0199" (ghost)
- Trust bar below: "Licensed & Insured · Response Within 1 Hour · Se Habla Español" — reuse from homepage but keep it here for reinforcement
- Height: 50-60vh desktop (shorter than homepage hero — this is a conversion page, not a brand page)

## Module 2: Pain Acknowledgment

**Purpose:** Show the visitor you understand their specific frustration. This is the empathy beat that earns the right to pitch solutions.

### Why This Matters
Most service company websites skip straight to "here's what we do." High-converting B2B pages start with "we know what you're dealing with." This creates an immediate sense of alignment — the visitor feels understood before being sold to.

### Content Per Persona

**General Contractors:**
> **The final clean shouldn't be the reason your closeout stalls.**
>
> Inconsistent subs. Missed walkthroughs. Punch-list callbacks for dust and debris that should have been handled. When the cleaning isn't right, your schedule pays for it — and so does your reputation with the owner.

**Property Managers:**
> **Slow turns cost more than cleaning ever will.**
>
> Every day a unit sits vacant between tenants is lost revenue. When cleaning is inconsistent, inspections fail, leasing tours get delayed, and your maintenance team ends up doing rework they shouldn't have to.

**Commercial Spaces:**
> **Your space is someone's first impression of your business.**
>
> Unreliable cleaning creates visible decline — dusty lobbies, stained break rooms, restrooms that drive complaints. And switching vendors feels like a hassle. Until the complaints get loud enough.

### Spec
- White/light background section
- Kicker: "THE PROBLEM" or "WHAT WE HEAR"
- Headline: bold, empathetic statement
- Body: 2-3 sentences maximum
- Optional: 2-3 bullet points listing specific pain signals
- No CTA in this section — it's a bridge, not a conversion point
- Padding: moderate (py-16 desktop)

## Module 5: Social Proof Block

**Purpose:** Persona-relevant testimonial paired with a stat bar. This is the section that converts skeptics.

### Why This Is Critical
Your homepage has testimonials, but they're generic to the brand. Each industry page needs **proof from someone in that persona's role**. A GC doesn't care what a property manager thinks. A PM doesn't care what an office manager thinks.

### What You Need Per Persona

**General Contractors:**
- Testimonial from a GC project manager or superintendent
- Stat bar: "200+ closeouts on schedule · Zero punch-list callbacks on cleaning · 15+ years field experience"
- If you don't have a real GC testimonial yet, use the Marcus Torres one from the homepage (tagged POST-CONSTRUCTION) and plan to replace it with a verified one

**Property Managers:**
- Testimonial from a PM or regional ops person
- Stat bar: "48hr average turnaround · 100% inspection pass rate · 500+ units turned"
- The James Rodriguez testimonial (tagged TURNOVER SUPPORT) on the homepage works here

**Commercial Spaces:**
- Testimonial from a facilities manager or office manager
- Stat bar: "15+ active facilities served weekly · Off-hours crews available · Recurring service available"
- You may not have a commercial-specific testimonial yet — this is a content gap to close

### Spec
- Dark background section (matches homepage testimonial section for brand consistency)
- Kicker: "VERIFIED RESULTS" or "FROM YOUR PEERS"
- Single large testimonial card (not a carousel — you only have/need one per persona)
- Card includes: star rating, quote text, name, title, company type
- Below testimonial: 3-stat bar (gold accent numbers, matching homepage Authority Bar style)
- If you don't have a real testimonial: use the closest match and add "[Industry] Client" as the tag. Replace when you collect real quotes post-launch.

## Module 7: How It Works (Simplified)

**Purpose:** Reduce the perceived effort of getting started. Show that the first step is easy.

### Structure
Three steps, not four. These pages are about converting, not educating. The homepage has the full 4-step process — here you just need to show it's simple.

### Content (Same Across All Personas — Adjust Language)

| Step | Title | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 01 | **Tell Us Your Scope** | Submit the form or call. Share your timeline, service need, and any specific requirements. |
| 02 | **We Scope & Quote** | We review your project, visit if needed, and deliver a clear quote — typically same day. |
| 03 | **We Deliver** | Crews arrive on schedule, work to standard, and you walk through a finished result. |

### Persona-Specific Adjustments
- **GCs:** Step 3 → "You Walk Through a Closeout-Ready Space"
- **PMs:** Step 3 → "Your Unit Is Lease-Ready"
- **Commercial:** Step 3 → "Your Space Stays Presentation-Ready"

### Spec
- Light background
- Horizontal 3-step layout on desktop, vertical on mobile
- Numbered steps with icons
- Kicker: "GETTING STARTED"
- Headline: "Three Steps to a Cleaner [Handoff / Turn / Space]"
- Below steps: CTA button "Request a Quote →"

## Module 8: FAQ Cluster

**Purpose:** Handle the 3-5 most common objections this specific persona has. This also has direct SEO value.

### Questions Per Persona

**General Contractors:**
1. "Can you match our construction schedule?" → Yes, we dispatch based on your CO date and walkthrough timeline. We've completed 200+ closeouts on schedule.
2. "Do you carry commercial insurance?" → Yes. COI available within 1 business hour. Coverage meets standard GC site requirements.
3. "What's included in a rough clean vs. final clean?" → [Detailed scope breakdown]
4. "How fast can you send a quote?" → Same-day for standard scopes. Within 24 hours for complex multi-phase projects.
5. "What happens if the walkthrough doesn't pass?" → We re-clean at no additional cost within 24 hours.

**Property Managers:**
1. "How fast can you turn a unit?" → Standard turnaround is 48 hours. Same-day available for emergency turns.
2. "Do you offer per-unit pricing?" → Yes. Flat rates by unit type (studio, 1BR, 2BR, 3BR). No hourly billing.
3. "Can you handle multiple units at once?" → Yes. We scale crew allocation to your seasonal volume.
4. "Do you clean common areas too?" → Yes. Lobbies, hallways, amenity spaces, and laundry rooms.
5. "What's your re-clean policy?" → If a unit doesn't pass inspection, we re-clean within 24 hours at no charge.

**Commercial Spaces:**
1. "Can you clean during off-hours?" → Yes. Evening, weekend, and overnight crews available.
2. "Do you handle both regular cleaning and deep cleans?" → Yes. We offer recurring maintenance plus quarterly/annual deep cleans under one scope.
3. "What types of commercial spaces do you serve?" → Offices, retail, medical, schools, churches, co-working spaces.
4. "Do we need a long-term contract?" → No. Month-to-month available. We earn your renewal every month.
5. "How do we get started?" → Request a quote or call. We'll schedule a walkthrough and propose a scope within 48 hours.

### Spec
- Accordion format (matching homepage FAQ style)
- Kicker: "COMMON QUESTIONS"
- Headline: "Questions [Persona Plural] Ask"
- Each answer: 50-100 words, specific and confident
- **Add FAQ schema (JSON-LD) per page** — each page gets its own unique FAQ schema
- Below accordion: "More questions? See our full FAQ →" linking to /faq

## Module 9: Conversion Block

**Purpose:** Terminal CTA for the page. Give them a clear, low-friction way to take the next step.

### Spec
Two options depending on page length:

**Option A: CTA Block (if page is long enough)**
- Dark background section
- Kicker: "READY TO START"
- Headline: persona-specific — e.g., "Let's get your closeout on schedule." / "Let's get your units turning faster." / "Let's keep your space running clean."
- Dual CTA: "Request a Quote →" (primary) + "Or call: (512) 555-0199" (secondary)
- 3 trust points: "Response within 1 hour · Licensed & Insured · Se Habla Español"

**Option B: Inline Form (higher conversion but longer page)**
- Embed a simplified version of the quote form directly on the page
- Pre-select the most likely service type based on the persona
- Fields: Name, Phone, Email, Service Type (pre-selected), Timeline, Notes
- This is the higher-performing option for paid traffic

### Recommendation
Use Option A for organic/referral visitors (most of your initial traffic). Switch to Option B when you start running persona-targeted ads — the inline form reduces friction for paid traffic where every click counts.

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| Don't Do This | Why | Instead |
|---|---|---|

| CTAs that require precision tapping | Mobile thumbs need bigger targets | Minimum 48px tap targets, full-width preferred |

# Part 1: Strategic Context

## Why Service Area Pages Matter Disproportionately

For a local service business, service area pages are among the highest-ROI pages you can build. Here's why:

### Search Behavior Reality

When a general contractor in Round Rock needs post-construction cleaning, they don't search "post-construction cleaning." They search:

- "post construction cleaning Round Rock TX"
- "construction cleanup near Round Rock"
- "cleaning company Round Rock Austin area"
- "final clean subcontractor Pflugerville"

**Every city you serve is a keyword modifier.** Without dedicated city pages, you're competing for one keyword set ("post construction cleaning Austin"). With city pages, you're competing for 10+ keyword sets — one per city — with dramatically less competition per term.

### The Local SEO Math

| Approach | Keywords Targeted | Competition Level |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage only | "post construction cleaning Austin" | High — every cleaning company in Austin targets this |
| Homepage + city pages | "post construction cleaning [city]" × 10 cities | Low to Medium — most competitors don't have dedicated city pages |
| Homepage + city pages + service-city combinations | "final clean Round Rock TX", "apartment turnover Georgetown TX" | Very Low — long-tail combinations with high purchase intent |

### What Google Wants to See

Google's local algorithm evaluates three factors for local pack ranking:

1. **Relevance** — Does your page content match the search query? (City name + service type in content)
2. **Proximity** — How close is your business to the searcher? (You can't change this, but city pages help Google understand your service radius)
3. **Prominence** — How established/authoritative are you for this area? (Dedicated pages with unique content signal expertise in each area)

A single "We serve these areas" list does almost nothing for local SEO. Dedicated pages with unique, area-specific content are what move the needle.

## Module 2: Why A&A in [City]

This is the section that makes each page unique and prevents Google from seeing them as template duplicates.

> **Why Georgetown Contractors and Property Teams Choose A&A**

### Why This Content Is Critical

| Without It | With It |
|---|---|
| Google sees a template page with just the city name swapped | Google sees unique content demonstrating genuine local expertise |
| Thin content penalty risk | Substantial page that can rank independently |
| No differentiation from competitors who also list "We serve Georgetown" | Content that a Georgetown-based PM or GC would find credible and relevant |

### Content Research for Each City

You don't need to be a Georgetown expert. Here's a 15-minute process per city:

1. **Google "[city] new construction projects"** — find 2-3 named developments, neighborhoods, or corridors
2. **Google "[city] commercial development"** — find any retail centers, office parks, or mixed-use projects
3. **Check the city's economic development page** — most Texas cities publish growth stats and development maps
4. **Reference major roads/corridors** — I-35, 183A, SH 130, FM roads that locals recognize
5. **Note any major employers or institutions** — universities (Texas State in San Marcos), corporate campuses (Dell in Round Rock), military (none here), hospital systems

This gives you enough local detail to write 150-250 words of genuinely unique, locally-grounded content per city.

# Part 1: Strategic Context

## Why the About Page Matters More Than You Think

For most SaaS companies, the About page is an afterthought — a place to put the team photo and founding story. For a **local service business selling to B2B buyers**, the About page is one of the most important pages on the site. Here's why:

### How Your Buyers Use the About Page

Your three personas — general contractors, property managers, and commercial space managers — all follow a similar vendor evaluation process:

```
1. Find the company (search, referral, jobsite sighting)
2. Check the homepage (do they do what I need?)
3. Check the About page (who are these people? can I trust them?)
4. Check proof (testimonials, reviews, portfolio)
5. Make contact (quote request, call)
```

**Step 3 is where the About page lives.** The visitor has already decided A&A offers the right services. Now they're evaluating the business itself:

- Is this a real company or a guy with a truck?
- How long have they been doing this?
- Do they have the operational capacity for my project?
- Are they professional enough to be on my jobsite?
- Can I put my reputation on the line by recommending them to my client?

That last question is the most important one. **A GC who hires A&A is staking their own credibility with the property owner.** A PM who brings in A&A is accountable to their management company. The About page needs to make the visitor confident enough to stake their professional reputation on this choice.

### What the Data Shows

For local service businesses:

| Metric | Source |
|---|---|
| About pages are the **2nd or 3rd most visited page** on most service business websites (after homepage, sometimes after services) | Multiple website analytics studies |
| **52% of users** say the About page is the first thing they look for when evaluating a new company | KoMarketing B2B Web Usability Report |
| For B2B buyers, **company credibility signals** outweigh pricing in vendor selection for services under $50K | Hinge Research Institute |

### What the Current State Is

The homepage About section contains:
- "Built on Standards" headline
- Two paragraphs of company philosophy
- Three stats: 500+ Projects Delivered, 6+ Years in Austin, 24hr Turnaround Capability
- A pull quote: "We don't leave until it's right."
- A CTA: "START YOUR PROJECT →"
- A "Licensed & Insured" badge
- An image that appears to be a conference/event setting (not directly related to cleaning)

This is a decent summary section for the homepage, but it's not substantial enough to stand alone as a page. The About page needs to be significantly deeper — telling a complete story that answers every question the buyer has about who A&A is and why they should trust them.

## Module 2: Origin Story

### Purpose
Tell the founding story in a way that builds credibility and emotional connection. This is the section that answers "Why does this company exist?" and "Why should I care?"

### Content Framework

The origin story should hit these beats in this order:

1. **The problem the founder saw firsthand** — inconsistent cleaning subs, failed walkthroughs, unreliable vendors
2. **The breaking point** — a specific moment or pattern that made starting A&A feel necessary
3. **The founding principle** — what A&A was built to be different about
4. **The growth** — from first project to current scale
5. **The present** — what A&A is today

### Draft Content

> **How A&A Started**
>
> Before A&A Cleaning was a company, it was a frustration.
>
> [Founder name] spent [X] years working in and around construction — seeing firsthand what happens when the cleaning sub doesn't show, shows up late, or delivers work that doesn't pass the walkthrough. The contractor takes the call from the owner. The property manager scrambles before the inspection. The deadline moves, and someone's reputation takes the hit.
>
> A&A was started to be the cleaning company that doesn't create those problems. One that shows up when scheduled, cleans to the standard the first time, and communicates like a professional trade partner — not a vendor you have to chase.
>
> What started as a small crew serving Austin-area construction sites has grown into a team delivering [500+] projects across the greater Austin metro — from Georgetown to San Marcos. The scope has expanded from post-construction cleanup to commercial cleaning, unit turnovers, and facility maintenance. The standard hasn't changed.

### Key Writing Principles

| Principle | Why |
|---|---|
| **Write in concrete terms, not abstractions** | "Shows up when scheduled" > "We value reliability" |
| **Name the pain before the solution** | The founder didn't start a company because they love cleaning. They started it because they saw a gap that hurt people. |
| **Avoid corporate clichés** | No "passion for excellence," no "committed to quality," no "customer-centric approach." Your buyer has seen those words on every competitor's site. |
| **Use construction-world language** | "Walkthrough," "punch list," "handoff," "closeout" — these words signal insider knowledge |
| **Keep it under 200 words** | This isn't a memoir. It's a trust-building bridge. Get in, tell the story, get out. |

#### Founder Bio Framework

The bio should cover:
1. **Background** — where they came from, what they did before A&A
2. **Why cleaning** — the connection to construction/facilities that makes this authentic
3. **How they lead** — hands-on, on-site, standards-driven
4. **Personal touch** — one human detail (family, Austin native, community involvement)

#### Draft Bio

> **[Name], Founder & Operations Lead**
>
> [Name] started A&A after [X] years working in and around Austin's construction industry — first in [role/trade], then managing cleanup operations for builders across the metro. That background shaped a simple operating principle: the job isn't done until the person writing the check would sign off on every surface.
>
> [Name] still oversees crew scheduling, quality standards, and client relationships directly. When there's a walkthrough, a complicated scope, or a first-time project with a new client, [they're] on-site.
>
> Outside of A&A, [one personal detail — lives in Austin with family, coaches youth sports, active in the Austin construction community, etc.].

#### Crew Section (Below Founder)

You probably don't want to list every crew member (turnover is real in this industry). Instead, describe the crew culture:

> **The A&A Crew**
>
> Every crew member is direct-hire — not subcontracted. Our teams train to a single quality standard and work under consistent supervision. Bilingual coordination (English and Spanish) is standard on every project.
>
> We value reliability, attention to detail, and professionalism on active jobsites. If that sounds like you, [we're hiring →](/careers).

This does four things:
1. Establishes that crews are direct (not subcontracted) — major trust signal for GCs
2. Reinforces bilingual capability
3. Signals operational discipline
4. Creates a natural link to the careers page
